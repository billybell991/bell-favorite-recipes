This is a mish-mash of a few different recipes for this I've seen on the web, along with a few different techniques.  No single one had all the ingredients and info in the same spot, so I figured I'd just write it up myself.  If it sucks, I'm sorry.  :(

## Ingredients

- 2 cups of flour (I just use regular flour, but the internet nerds say to use semolina and some other weird stuff.  Keep it simple!)
- 2 eggs
- 1/2 tsp olive oil
- 1/4 tsp salt
- 1/2 cup of water (just keep it handy - DO NOT just dump it in!!  See the recipe below for the deets)

NOTE: This is a doubled-up recipes that makes around 4 good portions with enough left over for lunch the next day.

1) In a big mixing bowl, add the flour and salt and make kind of a "well" in the middle of it.  Just a little dent that'll fit 2 eggs in it.  Crack the 2 eggs and the olive oil into the well.  (A lot of recipes say to do this right on the counter, which I get to, but this first step is a bit gummy so it's better to make the initial mess in a bowl than your counter)

2) With a fork, start working the edges of the well so the egg and flour come together.  It's going to seem like it's not coming together at all, but keep at it and it'll start kind of coming together.  It won't all come together and it'll seem like a big crusty mess for a bit.  Just add a splash of water (NOT THE WHOLE THING) here and there to have it come together better.  I even it's better to just wet my hands with the water, rather than just splashing it in.  This is where a bit of technique comes in.  Gotta play with it a bit.

3) When it's MOSTLY together (and it won't all be beautiful at this point), dump the contents of your bowl onto the counter.  Wipe the counter first, of course.  Don't do like me and have half of Maddy's Nutella toast stuck in your noodles.

4) This is where a bit more technique comes in.  It's still going to be a bit of a mess on your counter, but the goal is to get all those bits and flour-y shrapnel pieces combined.  Start kneading it the way you would with any other dough.  Add a little splash of water or just wet your hands with the water whenever you think it needs it.  The key is to get the dough so everything is incorporated, you can't see any flour, your counter is clean (even parts of Maddy's Nutella toast), and it's NOT sticking to your hands anymore.  It should be kinda moist, but not sticky, as gross as that sounds.  NOTE: You DO NOT have to use all the water.  It's just there if you need it.  Like a good friend.  The key here is that you want it to JUST be where everything's incorporated and parts aren't falling out.  Water usage will make or break this.  JUST enough to have things come together and that's it.  And put the time in, kneading it.  At least 7 mins, maybe more.  This is a patience thing and it's tiring (sadly...I'm out of shape, sue me.).

5) Once your dough is how I mentioned it should be above, form it into a big ball and wrap it in Saran Wrap and leave it on the counter.  Leave it for at least 30 mins, but it's better to shoot for 45-60 mins.  It's not rising during this time, like bread, but just forming all the science-y gluten-y goodness and things that make it easier to make into noodles later on.

6) Once you've relaxed for the equivalent of one episode of Game of Thrones or so, grab a dish towel or tea towel and moisten it.  Don't soak it, just moist.  God, I use that word more than I should.  Grab your dough ball and cut it into 8 equal pieces.  Put them inside your dish towel and cover them, so they don't dry out while you're working with one of them.

7) To make the actual noodles, I use one of those semi-cheapy jobbies you can get on Amazon for 50 bucks.  Just a normal pasta press that connects to your counter with a crank handle.

8) Grab one of the chunks of dough and roll it into a ball with your hands.  Then, give it a little squish with your hands so it's more of a puck.

9) Set your pasta maker to it's widest setting (mine is 7) and run your dough puck through it.  If the puck goes through once, then that's once more than we saw the Leafs put a puck through in these last playoffs.  Once it's flat, fold both sides of it in on itself, almost like it's crossing its arms.  Then run it back through, still on that widest setting.  Do that 1-2 more times, still on that widest setting.

10) Set the pasta maker to the next lowest setting and WITHOUT making the dough "cross its arms", run it through.  Just once.  Then, repeat for the next lowest setting and repeat, lower, repeat, lower till you're at the thickness of noodle you want.

11) Now, what I'll do here so noodles don't stick to themselves is I pre-dust them with flour.  Easiest way is while they're in this big sheet you just cranked out.  So, have a cutting board with a thin dusting of flour on it beside you.  Once your thin sheet of dough comes out, lay it on this cutting board of flour.  Sprinkle more flour on top of the sheet and smooth it out with your hand.  Not a lot, doesn't need a lot.  Just so it's not gummy.

12) I typically cut this dough sheet in half to make it easier to work with.  Then, just run it through your noodle cutting attachment.

Once that's all done, you can boil it for maybe 3-4 mins and eat it right away.  Don't overboil it!

The last batch I made, I let dry on a dry dish towel for a bit (formed into little "nests") and then put it into a container to freeze.  No clue how that'll turn out, but if it's a mess, then I'll update this recipe!  Haha!  Happy noodling!
